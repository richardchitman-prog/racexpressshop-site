The robocall tax on your parents

Phone scams don't cost everyone the same. For older adults, a single call is now the most expensive way a scammer can reach them — and the tactic isn't luck, it's isolation. Here's the pattern, and how RoboCallMeNot fits into helping without hovering.

Exhibit A

Why the phone is still the costliest way in

Scammers reach people through social media, email, and text — but the phone call is where the real damage happens. Total fraud losses reported by adults 60 and older climbed from about $600 million in 2020 to $2.4 billion in 2024, and in 2025 adults 50 and older reported $4.3 billion lost, compared with $2.3 billion for everyone younger.

That's not a small gap. A scam that opens with a ringing phone tends to produce more than three times the loss of one that opens on a social feed — because a live call is the fastest way to create urgency and keep someone from pausing to check the story with anyone else.

Exhibit B

The tactic is isolation, not gullibility

FTC research into these cases is clear that it isn't about someone being easily fooled — it's a deliberate script. Reports of older adults losing $10,000 or more to business and government imposter scams increased more than fourfold from 2020 to 2024. When the loss topped $100,000, the number of reports rose nearly sevenfold over the same period. In 2024, 41% of those high-loss imposter cases started with a phone call.

01

A fabricated emergency

Fraud on your account, a warrant, a family member in trouble. The story is built to demand a reaction before there's time to think.

02

Borrowed authority

The caller ID or script claims to be a bank, Social Security, Amazon, or law enforcement. Real agencies don't open with threats of immediate arrest.

03

Staying on the line

The longer the call runs, the less likely the person is to hang up and call a friend or family member who might see through it. That isolation is the actual weapon.

04

A payment that can't come back

Gift cards, wire transfers, and crypto kiosks show up again and again in these cases specifically because they're nearly impossible to reverse.

Exhibit C

How to help without hovering

Nobody wants to feel monitored by their own kids. The goal isn't to watch every call — it's to make logging and reporting a normal, shared habit before a scam call ever comes in.

01

Set it up together, once

Install RoboCallMeNot on their phone during a normal visit, not after something's already gone wrong. It takes a few minutes and it's theirs to use, not yours to check.

02

Agree on the rule in advance

No real agency or bank asks for gift cards, wire transfers, or crypto. Say that plainly, once, before any call — it's much easier to remember than to reason through mid-call.

03

Make reporting the default, not the exception

Logging a call in RoboCallMeNot takes one tap and doesn't require deciding first whether it was "serious enough." That removes the hesitation that lets patterns go undocumented.
